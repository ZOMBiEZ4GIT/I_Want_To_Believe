### THE CREDENTIAL PROBLEM

**Lazar Claims:**
- Master's degrees from MIT and Caltech
- Worked as physicist at Los Alamos National Lab

**What Investigators Found:**
- **MIT & Caltech:** No records of Lazar attending
- Yearbooks, registrar records, alumni directories: No Bob Lazar
- **Los Alamos:** Worked as **technician for contractor**, NOT physicist
- Phone directory lists him (1982), but as tech support, not scientist
- **Conclusion:** Lazar inflated/fabricated credentials

**Lazar's Defense:**
- Government erased records to discredit him
- **Problem:** Erasing university records would require conspiracy across institutions
- MIT, Caltech have no reason to cooperate with coverup

**Stanton Friedman Investigation (1997, updated 2011):**
- **Background:** Nuclear physicist, pro-UFO researcher (believer in UFO reality)
- **Investigation:** Investigated Lazar's credentials thoroughly

### THE ELEMENT 115 "PREDICTION"

**What Believers Say:** Lazar predicted Element 115 in 1989; discovered 2003

**What Skeptics Say:**
- Element 115's existence was ALREADY predicted by periodic table
- Scientists knew heavy elements possible; just hadn't synthesized yet
- Lazar could have guessed based on public science
- **More importantly:** Synthesized Element 115 is **HIGHLY UNSTABLE**

**The Reality of Moscovium (Element 115):**
- **Isotopes:** Known isotopes range from Mc-287 to Mc-290
- **All isotopes extremely radioactive**
- **Most stable isotope:** Moscovium-290
  - **Half-life:** Only **0.65 seconds (650 milliseconds)**
- **Second most stable:** Moscovium-289
  - **Half-life:** 250 milliseconds
- **Decay process:** Within fractions of seconds, undergoes radioactive decay to form nihonium (daughter nuclei)
- **Lazar's claim:** STABLE isotope that can power spacecraft
- **Reality:** **NO stable isotope exists**â€”element decays almost instantly
- **His prediction was fundamentally WRONG**

**Scientific Consensus:**
- Stable superheavy elements likely impossible
- Moscovium (Element 115) decays in milliseconds
- Can't power anythingâ€”too unstable to even hold in container
