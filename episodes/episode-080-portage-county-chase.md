## Cold Open: Portage County, Ohio, April 17, 1966, 5:00 AM

Deputy Dale Spaur and Deputy Wilbur "Barney" Neff are parked on Route 224 near Atwater, Ohio, finishing paperwork on an abandoned car. It's predawn, still dark, the quiet hours when nothing happens.

Then Spaur sees something rising from the trees.

At first, he thinks it's a fire. A bright glow, yellow-white, illuminating the forest. But it's moving—rising straight up, silently.

"Barney, you seeing this?"

Neff looks up. His eyes widen.

The object clears the treeline. It's enormous—40 to 50 feet wide, shaped like an ice cream cone tipped on its side. The larger end glows brilliantly. The narrow end is dark, metallic. The whole thing hovers, perfectly silent.

Then it moves. Toward them.

Spaur grabs the radio. "Portage County, this is Car 13. We have a... we have an unidentified flying object."

Dispatcher Robert Wilson responds: "13, repeat?"

"We are observing an unidentified flying object. Large, metallic, extremely bright. Currently hovering about 150 feet over our position."

The object stops directly above their cruiser. The glow is so intense they can read a newspaper by it. No sound. No wind. Just light and silence.

Neff is terrified. "Dale, what do we do?"

Spaur doesn't know. What's protocol for this?

Then the object moves. East. Fast. But not too fast to follow.

Spaur makes a decision. "We're following it."

"Dale, are you serious?"

"Get in the car!"

They radio dispatch: "Portage County, Car 13 in pursuit of unidentified object, eastbound on 224."

Wilson, the dispatcher, doesn't believe what he's hearing. But the radio traffic is real. The deputies sound shaken but lucid. He patches in other units.

What follows is an 85-mile high-speed chase through three counties and into Pennsylvania. Two deputies pursuing a glowing object that stays just ahead of them, sometimes slowing down as if waiting, sometimes accelerating beyond their ability to follow.

En route, they're joined by Officer Wayne Huston of East Palestine, Ohio, who sees the object and confirms the pursuit. In Pennsylvania, Officer Frank Panzanella observes the object hovering as the Ohio deputies arrive, exhausted and shaken.

The chase ends at dawn when the object shoots straight up and disappears.

The deputies return home. Their lives will never be the same.

Within days, the Air Force explains it: they chased Venus. A bright planet on the horizon, misidentified due to fatigue and imagination.

The deputies know what they saw. They chased it for 85 miles. Multiple officers witnessed it. Dispatch recorded the whole thing.

But the official explanation stands: Venus.

And Dale Spaur, a decorated deputy with an unblemished record, becomes the laughingstock of Portage County. His marriage fails. His career ends. He spirals into depression.

For doing what police officers are supposed to do: report what they observe.

Welcome to the Portage County UFO chase—the case where credible witnesses did everything right, and it destroyed them anyway.

## Overview

The Portage County UFO chase occurred in the early morning hours of April 17, 1966, when Portage County, Ohio sheriff's deputies Dale Spaur and Wilbur Neff encountered a large, brightly glowing object that they pursued for approximately 85 miles from Atwater, Ohio to Freedom, Pennsylvania.

**Timeline of Events:**

- **5:00 AM:** Deputies Spaur and Neff observe large glowing object rising from wooded area
- **5:05 AM:** Object hovers over their patrol car; they begin pursuit eastbound
- **5:15 AM:** Pursuit continues through Ravenna, Ohio; object maintains position ahead of cruiser
- **5:30 AM:** Officer Wayne Huston of East Palestine PD joins pursuit, confirms object
- **5:45 AM:** Pursuit enters Pennsylvania
- **5:50 AM:** Conway, PA Officer Frank Panzanella observes object hovering
- **6:00 AM:** Object accelerates straight up and disappears as dawn breaks

**Key Evidence:**

- **Four police officers** (Spaur, Neff, Huston, Panzanella) witnessed object
- **Dispatch recordings** document real-time pursuit
- **Citizens** along the route reported seeing bright object
- **Consistent descriptions** from independent witnesses
- **Physical effects:** Spaur and Neff reported extreme fatigue after encounter

**Official Explanation:**

Project Blue Book concluded the officers misidentified Venus, possibly with added confusion from aircraft or other celestial objects.

**The Believer's Case argues:** Four trained police officers pursued a structured object for 85 miles. Venus doesn't hover overhead, doesn't move along highways, doesn't pace vehicles, and doesn't shoot straight up and disappear. The witnesses' lives were destroyed by ridicule despite consistent, corroborated testimony. The official explanation is insulting and impossible. This is one of the strongest multiple-witness UFO cases with credible observers who had nothing to gain and everything to lose.

**The Skeptic's Response counters:** Bright planets and stars, especially when observed through moving car windshields and under fatigue, can create powerful illusions of movement and proximity. The "chase" was officers driving toward a celestial object on the horizon that appeared to stay ahead due to parallax and distance. The dramatic psychological state (adrenaline, excitement, fear) enhanced misperception. Other witnesses saw bright lights consistent with celestial objects, not structured craft. The tragedy of Spaur's life doesn't make his perception accurate—it makes it a cautionary tale about the power of misperception and social consequences.

This episode examines one of the most famous police UFO cases and asks: Can credible witnesses be credibly wrong? And what's the cost of being a witness—right or wrong—to something incredible?

## The Believer's Case (25 minutes)

### The Credibility of Witnesses

Dale Spaur was not a UFO enthusiast. He was a 34-year-old sheriff's deputy with five years of law enforcement experience and a clean record. Barney Neff was a respected officer. Wayne Huston and Frank Panzanella had similar credentials.

These were trained observers whose job was to assess situations accurately and report them clearly. On any other morning, their testimony would be accepted without question.

**What makes police officer testimony compelling:**

- **Training in observation:** Officers are trained to observe details accurately for reports and testimony
- **Credibility stakes:** Law enforcement careers depend on reliable reporting
- **Sobriety and mental state:** Officers on duty are (should be) alert and clear-headed
- **Radio documentation:** Dispatch records confirm real-time reporting, not after-the-fact fabrication
- **No incentive to lie:** Officers gain nothing from UFO reports and risk ridicule and career damage

Spaur and Neff had every reason to minimize what they saw, every incentive to call it a plane or a star and move on. Instead, they reported an unidentified object and pursued it.

Why would they do this if they weren't seeing something extraordinary?

### The Object's Characteristics

The deputies' descriptions were detailed and consistent:

**Physical Description:**

- **Size:** 40-50 feet wide (close estimation based on proximity)
- **Shape:** Ice cream cone on its side, or bullet/torpedo shape
- **Structure:** Metallic surface on narrow end, brilliant glow on wide end
- **Brightness:** So intense they could read by it when overhead
- **Sound:** Completely silent
- **Movement:** Smooth, controlled, responsive

**Behavioral Characteristics:**

The object didn't just hover—it appeared to interact:

- **Hovering:** Stopped directly over patrol car, maintaining position
- **Pacing:** Stayed ahead of pursuing vehicle, adjusting speed
- **Awareness:** Seemed to "wait" when officers fell behind, accelerated when they caught up
- **Departure:** Shot straight up at incredible speed when dawn broke

This is not passive celestial object behavior. This is controlled, intelligent response to pursuit.

### The Pursuit: 85 Miles of Consistent Observation

The chase lasted approximately one hour over 85 miles through multiple jurisdictions. This wasn't a brief glimpse or momentary confusion—it was sustained observation under varying conditions:

**Phase 1: Initial Encounter (Atwater, Ohio)**

Object rises from trees, hovers over patrol car. Officers report brilliant glow, complete silence, structured metallic appearance.

At this point, the object is close—deputies estimated 150 feet overhead. This is not distant Venus on the horizon.

**Phase 2: Highway Pursuit (Route 224/14)**

Object moves east along highway. Deputies pursue at speeds up to 105 mph. Object maintains position ahead—close enough to track clearly, far enough to stay out of reach.

Dispatch recordings capture Spaur's real-time reports. His voice is excited but controlled, giving position updates and describing the object's movements.

**Phase 3: East Palestine, Ohio**

Officer Wayne Huston, monitoring the radio traffic, positions himself to observe. When Spaur and Neff arrive, Huston sees the object and confirms their description.

Huston joins the pursuit briefly, then breaks off to return to his jurisdiction. But he later testified: "I saw what they were chasing. It was no Venus. It was a bright object that moved in ways aircraft don't move."

**Phase 4: Pennsylvania Border**

The pursuit enters Pennsylvania. Officer Frank Panzanella of Conway, PA, having heard radio traffic, looks for the object. He sees it hovering and observes the Ohio deputies arrive beneath it.

Panzanella later testified: "It was shaped like an ice cream cone, with the large end glowing. It was definitely a structured object."

**Phase 5: Dawn Departure**

As daylight increases, the object suddenly accelerates straight up and disappears into the sky.

Spaur and Neff are exhausted, shaken, and nearly out of gas. They've driven 85 miles in pursuit of something they can't explain.

### The Dispatch Recordings

The radio traffic from that morning still exists. It documents:

- Real-time reports of the object's position and movement
- Spaur's descriptions matching later testimony
- Dispatcher's skepticism turning to concern as pursuit continues
- Multiple officers attempting to position for visual confirmation

This isn't reconstructed memory years later. This is contemporaneous reporting by trained officers actively observing.

The radio transcripts show Spaur reporting specific maneuvers: "It's turning... now it's hovering... now it's moving east again."

These reports match the behavior of a controlled object, not a stationary planet.

### The Corroborating Witnesses

Beyond the four police officers, citizens along the route reported seeing unusual lights that morning:

- Ravenna residents saw bright object moving east
- Witnesses in multiple towns reported object matching the timing of the pursuit
- Some described structured appearance, not just a light

While not all citizen witnesses provided detailed testimony, the pattern of reports along the pursuit route corroborates that something visible was moving eastward during the chase.

### The Physical and Psychological Effects

Both Spaur and Neff reported extreme fatigue after the encounter—unusual exhaustion beyond what an hour of driving would cause.

Some researchers note this pattern in close UFO encounters: witnesses report fatigue, time distortion, and physical effects suggesting exposure to unusual environmental factors (electromagnetic fields, radiation, other unknown emissions).

While not definitive, the reported fatigue adds another layer to the physical reality of the encounter.

### The Project Blue Book "Explanation"

The Air Force's explanation was insulting: The officers misidentified Venus, possibly with additional confusion from aircraft or satellites.

**Problems with this explanation:**

1. **Venus doesn't hover overhead:** Venus is a planetary object millions of miles away. It doesn't stop directly over observers at 150 feet altitude.

2. **Venus doesn't move along highways:** The object maintained position ahead of the pursuing vehicle, adjusting to their speed. Venus doesn't track with ground vehicles.

3. **Venus doesn't change altitude:** The object was observed at various altitudes, sometimes overhead, sometimes lower on the horizon. Venus maintains fixed position relative to celestial sphere.

4. **Venus doesn't accelerate upward:** The object shot straight up and disappeared. Venus doesn't accelerate—it maintains its orbit.

5. **Multiple officers misidentified in same way?** Four trained observers in different locations independently described the same characteristics. Mass misidentification of Venus to this degree strains credulity.

6. **Dispatch recordings don't match Venus:** Real-time reports describe movement patterns incompatible with celestial objects.

The Venus explanation requires believing that four police officers, trained in observation, spending an hour in active pursuit, couldn't distinguish between a planet and something overhead.

### The Life-Destroying Consequences

What happened to Dale Spaur after the encounter is tragedy:

- **Ridicule:** He became the town joke—"the cop who chased Venus"
- **Media harassment:** Reporters camped outside his home
- **Marriage collapse:** His wife left him, unable to handle the attention and mockery
- **Career destruction:** He resigned from the sheriff's department
- **Depression:** He spiraled into psychological crisis
- **Poverty:** Unable to maintain steady employment due to notoriety

In interviews years later, Spaur was bitter: "I saw something. I don't know what it was, but I know it wasn't Venus. I did my job. I reported what I saw. And it destroyed my life."

**The Believer's Argument:**

If Spaur had seen Venus and convinced himself it was something else, discovering his error would have brought relief. He could have said, "I was tired, I made a mistake, my bad."

Instead, he maintained for the rest of his life that he saw something real, something extraordinary. He paid an enormous price for this testimony—and he never recanted.

Why hold onto a story that destroys your life unless it's true?

### Why This Case Matters

The Portage County chase represents:

- **Multiple credible witnesses:** Four police officers
- **Extended duration:** One hour of continuous observation
- **Long distance:** 85 miles of pursuit
- **Contemporaneous documentation:** Dispatch recordings
- **Corroborating witnesses:** Citizens along the route
- **Consistent descriptions:** Matching details from independent observers
- **Professional cost:** Witnesses suffered career and personal damage

This is as strong as witness testimony gets. Trained observers, documented reporting, corroboration, consistency, and witnesses with nothing to gain and everything to lose.

If this testimony isn't credible, then witness testimony can never be credible for UFO cases.

## The Skeptic's Response (22 minutes)

### The Venus Explanation: More Plausible Than It Sounds

The Air Force's Venus explanation initially sounds absurd. But understanding the psychology and optics of celestial observation makes it surprisingly plausible.

**Venus in April 1966:**

- Venus was visible in the eastern predawn sky
- It was exceptionally bright (magnitude -4, the brightest object in the sky besides the Moon)
- It was positioned low on the eastern horizon, exactly where the pursuit headed
- Atmospheric conditions can make Venus appear to shimmer, change color, and seem larger than normal

**The Autokinetic Effect:**

When staring at a single bright point of light (like Venus or a star) against a dark sky, especially through a moving car windshield, the brain creates illusion of movement. This is called the autokinetic effect.

The object appears to:

- Move erratically
- Change position
- Follow the observer
- Respond to movements

This is a well-documented psychological phenomenon, not speculation.

**The Highway Pursuit Illusion:**

When driving toward a distant object on the horizon (like Venus), it creates a powerful illusion:

- The object appears to stay ahead of you, maintaining distance
- Speed up, and the object seems to speed up
- Slow down, and it seems to slow down
- Turn, and it seems to turn with you

This is parallax effect combined with the brain's interpretation of pursuit. The "chase" is an illusion created by driving toward a fixed celestial object.

### The "Overhead" Misperception

Spaur reported the object hovering directly overhead at close range. How does this reconcile with Venus low on the horizon?

**Answer: Angular perception distortion.**

In predawn darkness, without reference points, estimating altitude and distance is nearly impossible. A bright light can appear closer and higher than it actually is—a phenomenon well-known to pilots and called the "loom effect."

Additionally, if the initial sighting involved a different object (aircraft landing lights, satellite, etc.) that was genuinely overhead, this could have transitioned to pursuit of Venus as the original stimulus moved away and Venus became the focal point.

The human brain seeks continuity. If you're looking for a UFO and Venus is the brightest thing in the sky, your brain interprets Venus as the continuation of what you initially saw.

### The Multiple Witness "Confirmation"

Four officers saw something. But did they all see the same thing? And did any of them see what they thought they saw?

**Officer Wayne Huston:**

Huston positioned himself to observe after hearing radio traffic. He expected to see a UFO. When he looked east and saw a bright object, he confirmed "I see it."

But confirmation bias is powerful. Huston knew what Spaur was chasing. He looked east. He saw Venus (the brightest object in that direction). His brain interpreted: "That's the UFO."

**Officer Frank Panzanella:**

Similarly, Panzanella heard the radio traffic, looked east, saw bright object. His description of "ice cream cone shape" matches how Venus can appear when low on the horizon with atmospheric distortion creating an elongated or teardrop shape.

**The Problem:**

None of the confirming officers observed the object independent of context. They all heard about the UFO first, then looked for it, then "confirmed" the brightest object they saw.

This is not independent corroboration—it's social reinforcement of shared misperception.

### The Dispatch Recordings: Real Pursuit, Wrong Target

The dispatch recordings prove the officers genuinely believed they were chasing something. They don't prove the something was extraordinary.

The transcripts show:

- Officers reporting object position
- Describing movements
- Expressing excitement and confusion

All of this is consistent with officers genuinely pursuing what they perceived to be a UFO—while actually pursuing Venus through a combination of autokinetic effect, pursuit illusion, and heightened psychological state.

**The officers weren't lying.** They were experiencing a powerful perceptual illusion.

### The Psychological State: Adrenaline and Perception

Consider the deputies' mental state:

- **Predawn hours:** Fatigue, end of shift, biological low point
- **Unexpected stimulus:** Bright light, no immediate explanation
- **Adrenaline surge:** "What is this?"
- **Decision to pursue:** Commitment to the chase
- **Escalating excitement:** Radio traffic, other officers joining, pursuit across state lines
- **Social reinforcement:** Other officers "confirming" the sighting

This progression creates a psychological state where:

- Critical thinking diminishes
- Perception narrows to focus on the target
- Alternative explanations become difficult to consider
- The narrative becomes fixed: "We're chasing a UFO"

Once committed to this interpretation, disconfirming evidence (the object's behavior matching a celestial object, the lack of structured details when close, the perfect timing with Venus position) is filtered out.

This isn't stupidity or dishonesty. It's normal human psychology under stress and excitement.

### The Citizen Witnesses: Predictable Patterns

Citizens along the route saw bright lights. This is unsurprising:

- Venus was visible to anyone looking east
- News of a "UFO chase" spread quickly via radio and word of mouth
- People looked for UFOs and saw the brightest object (Venus)
- Memory of "seeing a UFO" formed through combination of actual Venus observation and expectation

None of the citizen testimony provides details incompatible with Venus observation. They saw bright lights—which Venus was that morning.

### The Physical Effects: Post-Event Fatigue

Spaur and Neff reported extreme fatigue after the encounter. Skeptics explain this as:

- **Adrenaline crash:** Hour of high-stress pursuit followed by adrenaline depletion
- **Psychological exhaustion:** Emotional intensity, confusion, uncertainty
- **Actual fatigue:** End of night shift, predawn hours, biological low point
- **Disappointment:** Realization that they didn't catch what they were chasing

These factors adequately explain fatigue without requiring exposure to exotic energies.

### The Project Blue Book Explanation: Harsh But Correct

The Air Force's Venus explanation was delivered insensitively and contributed to the officers' ridicule. That was wrong.

But the explanation itself is likely correct.

**What probably happened:**

1. **Initial stimulus:** Aircraft landing lights, satellite, or other conventional object briefly visible
2. **Officers investigate:** Look for source, see Venus rising in east
3. **Pursuit begins:** Drive toward Venus, creating illusion of chase
4. **Perception locks in:** Brain interprets Venus as UFO, filters disconfirming evidence
5. **Social reinforcement:** Other officers "confirm" Venus as the UFO
6. **Dawn departure:** Venus becomes difficult to see as sky brightens, appears to "shoot up" (actually just fading from view)

This sequence requires no exotic technology, no alien craft, no physics violations.

It requires only:

- A bright planet
- Optical and psychological illusions well-documented in literature
- Normal human perception under stress
- Social reinforcement of shared misperception

All of these are common and well-understood.

### The Tragedy of Dale Spaur

Spaur's life destruction is genuinely tragic. But it doesn't validate his perception.

**What destroyed Spaur:**

- Media harassment and sensationalism
- Community ridicule
- Institutional failure to support their officer
- His own inability to accept that he might have been mistaken

If the Air Force and community had said: "You saw something bright and unusual. You pursued it doing your job. Venus can create powerful illusions. No shame in this. Thank you for your service"—Spaur's life might not have collapsed.

Instead, he was mocked. And in response, he doubled down on his interpretation, unable to admit possible error because that would mean his suffering was for nothing.

**The Skeptical Point:**

Spaur's suffering was real. His sincerity was real. His observation was real.

But Venus creates illusions powerful enough to fool trained observers, especially under the exact conditions (predawn, highway pursuit, adrenaline, social reinforcement) present that morning.

Tragedy doesn't equal validation. Sincerity doesn't equal accuracy.

### The Pattern of Police UFO Encounters

The Portage County case follows a pattern common in police UFO reports:

- Predawn or nighttime
- Bright celestial object (Venus, Jupiter, bright stars)
- Moving vehicle creating parallax effects
- Radio traffic creating social reinforcement
- Heightened psychological state
- Later insistence that "it couldn't have been a star"

This pattern has been documented dozens of times. And when investigated, most resolve to celestial misidentification.

Police officers are trained in many things. Celestial navigation and astronomical observation aren't among them.

### What This Case Actually Demonstrates

The Portage County chase demonstrates:

- **How powerful perceptual illusions can be:** Four trained observers experienced the same illusion
- **How social reinforcement works:** Officers confirming each other's observations
- **How commitment to interpretation persists:** Despite evidence for Venus, witnesses maintain UFO interpretation
- **How institutions handle controversial claims:** Poorly, with ridicule instead of empathy
- **How witnesses suffer:** Real personal costs for reporting unusual observations

These are important lessons. But they're about human psychology and institutional response, not about alien spacecraft.

## My Take (8 minutes)

The Portage County UFO chase breaks my heart. And I think both believers and skeptics miss important elements of why.

### What I Think Happened

I lean toward the skeptical explanation: The deputies pursued Venus under conditions that created powerful illusions of a controlled object.

This doesn't mean they were stupid, dishonest, or bad observers. It means they experienced a well-documented perceptual phenomenon that can fool anyone under the right conditions.

**The factors aligned perfectly:**

- Bright Venus in exact position
- Predawn darkness removing reference points
- Highway pursuit creating parallax illusion
- Autokinetic effect creating apparent movement
- Adrenaline and excitement narrowing perception
- Social reinforcement from other officers
- Commitment to "UFO chase" interpretation

Under these conditions, Venus really can create the experience of chasing a controlled object.

### What Really Disturbs Me

But here's what troubles me about this case—it's not what happened in the sky. It's what happened on the ground.

**Dale Spaur did exactly what law enforcement officers should do:**

- Observed something unusual
- Investigated
- Reported clearly and accurately what he perceived
- Followed procedure

For this, his life was destroyed. Not by the UFO encounter itself, but by:

- Media sensationalism
- Community ridicule
- Institutional abandonment
- Official dismissiveness

The Air Force could have said: "The officers observed Venus under conditions known to create powerful illusions. This is understandable and involves no incompetence." Instead, the explanation was delivered in ways that made the officers seem foolish.

The community could have said: "Our deputies did their job." Instead, Spaur became the town joke.

The institution could have supported their officer. Instead, they let him twist in the wind.

**The Real Tragedy:**

Spaur didn't lose everything because he saw a UFO. He lost everything because of how people reacted to him reporting what he perceived.

And that's inexcusable regardless of what was actually in the sky.

### The Believer's Mistake

Believers frame this case as definitive proof because of credible witnesses, corroboration, and personal cost to testify.

But they miss that:

- Credible witnesses can experience powerful illusions
- Corroboration can be social reinforcement of shared misperception
- Personal cost doesn't validate accuracy of perception

The strength of belief and the severity of consequences don't determine truth. They determine the importance of the experience to the individual.

### The Skeptic's Mistake

Skeptics correctly identify the probable explanation (Venus + illusions) but often minimize the genuineness of the experience and the tragedy of the consequences.

Saying "it was just Venus" misses that:

- The illusion was real and powerful
- The officers' confusion and fear were legitimate
- The social consequences were devastating
- The case teaches important lessons about perception and institutional response

Dismissing this as "cops fooled by a planet" is as problematic as claiming it's proof of aliens.

### What I Wish Had Happened

I wish the response had been:

**From the Air Force:** "Thank you for your report. You observed Venus under atmospheric and psychological conditions that can create powerful illusions of movement and structure. This is a documented phenomenon, and your reporting helps us understand how it affects trained observers. No criticism of your professionalism is intended."

**From the community:** "Our deputies did their job. They investigated and reported. We're proud of their professionalism."

**From the media:** Responsible coverage acknowledging both the interesting perceptual phenomenon and the officers' credibility.

If that had happened, maybe Dale Spaur's life wouldn't have collapsed. Maybe he could have processed the experience without needing to defend it for life. Maybe the case would be remembered as an important lesson about perception rather than a cautionary tale about ridicule.

### What This Case Teaches

The Portage County chase is valuable not as proof of UFOs but as a demonstration of:

**How perception works:** Powerful illusions can affect anyone
**How social dynamics work:** Group reinforcement of shared perception
**How institutions fail:** Ridicule instead of support
**How consequences matter:** Real people suffer real costs
**How complexity matters:** Not every case is simple, even when explanations exist

### My Bottom Line

I think the deputies chased Venus. I think they genuinely experienced it as something extraordinary. I think their testimony is sincere. And I think their treatment afterward was shameful.

All of these can be true simultaneously.

The truth was in the sky: probably Venus.

But the greater truth is on the ground: how we treat people who report unusual experiences matters enormously, regardless of what they actually observed.

Dale Spaur deserved better. Not because he was right about seeing a UFO, but because he was honest about his experience and professional in his reporting.

The tragedy isn't that he chased Venus. The tragedy is that honesty destroyed him.

And that's a truth that has nothing to do with what's out there—and everything to do with what's in here: how we treat each other when faced with uncertainty, strangeness, and the limits of understanding.
